How to Read a Raag Profile

Aaroh / Avaroh

The specific notes used going up (Aaroh) and down (Avaroh). These are not always identical — the asymmetry is what gives each raag its unique shape and personality. Learn these first when studying any raag.

Swar Notation

Plain pill = Shuddha (natural note). Red pill = Komal ♭ (flattened). Orange pill = Tivra ↑ (sharpened Ma only). Sa' with apostrophe = upper octave.

Vadi & Samvadi

Vadi = the King note — the most emphasised swara, dwelt upon most. Samvadi = the Prime Minister — second most important. Together they give the raag its emotional centre. Always a 4th or 5th apart.

Jati

The number of notes in Aaroh / Avaroh. Audav = 5 notes, Shadav = 6, Sampurna = 7. E.g. "Audav-Sampurna" means 5 notes ascending, 7 notes descending.

Time & Season

Each raag has an ideal time of day and often a season. This is based on centuries of observation that certain note combinations feel natural at certain times. You can play any raag at any time — but at its prescribed time, it resonates most deeply.

Difficulty

★ = Beginner (few notes, clear rules), ★★ = Intermediate, ★★★ = Advanced (complex rules, many similar raags to distinguish). Difficulty reflects learning complexity, not emotional depth.

Raag Yaman
राग यमन
Kalyan Thaat
Evening
6–9 PM
★★★★★ Beginner
What is this raag? Yaman is the traditional first raag taught to students — it uses all 7 swaras with one change: Tivra (raised) Ma instead of Shuddha Ma. Its mood is peaceful devotion with a sense of longing — like watching a beautiful sunset. It is the most-composed raag in Hindustani music.
Aaroh (Ascent) — starts from lower Ni, not Sa
NiReGaMa↑DhaNiSa'
Avaroh (Descent)
Sa'NiDhaMa↑GaReSa
Vadi: Ga
Samvadi: Ni
Jati: Sampurna–Sampurna
Pakad: Ni Re Ga Ma↑ Dha Ni
Peaceful, devotional, romantic yearning. The raised Ma creates a bright question — like anticipating something beautiful. Associated with Meera Bai's devotional poetry.

Why does Yaman sound this way? The Tivra (raised) Ma is the key. It sits between Shuddha Ma and Pa, creating a bright, slightly unstable tension that always wants to resolve upward. This gives Yaman its characteristic feeling of anticipation and longing — never quite settled, always reaching.

Raag Bhupali
राग भूपाली
Kalyan Thaat
Evening
6–9 PM
★★★★★ Beginner
What is this raag? Bhupali is a pentatonic raag — only 5 notes, no Ma, no Ni. This simplicity makes it one of the purest and most serene raags. Often the second raag a student learns after Yaman. It sounds like "pure joy" — clean, open, uncluttered. Many folk songs and film songs are based on Bhupali.
Aaroh
SaReGaPaDhaSa'
Avaroh
Sa'DhaPaGaReSa
Vadi: Ga
Samvadi: Dha
Jati: Audav–Audav (5 notes)
Serene, pure, open-hearted joy. The absence of Ma and Ni removes tension and complexity — what remains is clarity. Like a cloudless sky.

Why does removing Ma and Ni create serenity? Ma and Ni are the two most "tense" swaras — they create the most yearning and longing. By removing them, Bhupali has no tension points. Every note is stable and consonant, producing the characteristic open, peaceful sound. This is also why the pentatonic scale appears in children's music worldwide — the brain experiences it as uncomplicated and welcoming.

Raag Bhairav
राग भैरव
Bhairav Thaat
Sunrise
6 AM
★★★★★ Beginner
What is this raag? Bhairav is sung at sunrise — the most solemn and devotional time of day. It is named after Shiva in his fierce form, but the mood is not fierce — it is deeply still, meditative, and spiritual. The Komal Re and Komal Dha give it a heaviness that matches the quiet gravity of early morning.
Aaroh
SaRe♭GaMaPaDha♭NiSa'
Avaroh
Sa'NiDha♭PaMaGaRe♭Sa
Vadi: Dha♭
Samvadi: Re♭
Jati: Sampurna–Sampurna
Profoundly meditative, solemn, devotional. Like standing alone in a temple at dawn — the world is still, the mind is quiet, the heart is full.

Why is this a morning raag? The Komal Re (flat Re) and Komal Dha (flat Dha) create a lowered, muted quality — the same quality as early morning light, which is soft and indirect. Ancient musicians observed that the human nervous system is most receptive to these soft, heavy pitches in the early hours, when the mind is still half in sleep.

Raag Kafi
राग काफी
Kafi Thaat
Night
9 PM–12 AM
★★★★★ Intermediate
What is this raag? Kafi is the raag of romance and folk feeling. Its Komal Ga and Komal Ni give it a quality very close to the Western minor scale — accessible, emotional, and deeply human. Many Holi songs and folk melodies are based on Kafi. Thumri and Dadra singers love it for its expressive flexibility.
Aaroh
SaReGa♭MaPaDhaNi♭Sa'
Avaroh
Sa'Ni♭DhaPaMaGa♭ReSa
Vadi: Pa
Samvadi: Sa
Jati: Sampurna–Sampurna
Romantic, bittersweet, folk-like. The Komal Ga gives it warmth and slight sadness — like remembering a happy moment that has passed.

Why is Kafi used for Holi songs? The Komal Ga and Komal Ni create a "folk" quality — these notes appear frequently in folk music across North India. The Holi celebration is joyful but also bittersweet (associated with Radha-Krishna's longing). Kafi's minor-tinged but rhythmically lively character matches this perfectly.

Raag Darbari Kanada
राग दरबारी कानडा
Asavari Thaat
Deep Night
12–3 AM
★★★★★ Advanced
What is this raag? Darbari Kanada is one of the most powerful and revered raags in Hindustani music. Created by the legendary Miyan Tansen for Emperor Akbar's court (Darbar = royal court), it conveys royal grandeur, deep introspection, and a kind of majestic sorrow. Its signature is the distinctive "andolan" (slow oscillation) on Komal Ga.
Aaroh
SaReGa♭MaPaDha♭Ni♭Sa'
Avaroh
Sa'Ni♭Dha♭PaMaGa♭ReSa
Vadi: Re
Samvadi: Pa
Jati: Sampurna–Sampurna
Signature: Andolan on Ga♭
Royal, austere, majestic. Like sitting alone in an enormous throne room at midnight — the grandeur is overwhelming, the silence is profound. Not performed at casual events — reserved for serious classical concerts.

The andolan on Komal Ga: In Darbari, the Komal Ga is not sung cleanly — it is performed with a slow, deliberate oscillation between two nearby shrutis. This "searching" quality on Ga is what distinguishes Darbari from all other Asavari-thaat raags. Without this andolan, Darbari loses its identity entirely.

Raag Bhimpalasi
राग भीमपलासी
Kafi Thaat
Afternoon
3–6 PM
★★★★★ Intermediate
What is this raag? Bhimpalasi is an afternoon raag with a deeply emotional character — a sense of gentle longing, like missing someone you love. It uses only 5 notes going up (pentatonic ascent) but all 7 descending, creating an asymmetry that feels like sighing. Very popular — many beloved bhajans and film songs are based on it.
Aaroh (5 notes — no Re or Dha)
SaGa♭MaPaNi♭Sa'
Avaroh (all 7 notes)
Sa'Ni♭DhaPaMaGa♭ReSa
Vadi: Ma
Samvadi: Sa
Jati: Audav–Sampurna
Tender longing, empathy, devotional love. Like the light of late afternoon — still warm but already edged with the melancholy of ending. The Komal Ga is its heart.

Why skip Re and Dha going up? The aaroh of Bhimpalasi skips Re and Dha — jumping from Sa directly to Komal Ga, then to Ma, Pa, Komal Ni, Sa'. This leap creates a forward-rushing energy on the way up — a sense of urgency and reaching. The full 7-note descent then sighs back down slowly, creating the characteristic "inhale-exhale" feeling of the raag.

Raag Todi
राग तोड़ी
Todi Thaat
Morning
9 AM–12 PM
★★★★★ Advanced
What is this raag? Todi is considered the most complex and profound morning raag — the "king" of morning raags. It has three Komal swaras AND a Tivra Ma — the most complex combination in Hindustani music. Legend says Tansen's daughter Saraswati used Todi to cure her father's illness. Masters spend years perfecting it.
Aaroh
SaRe♭Ga♭Ma↑PaDha♭NiSa'
Avaroh
Sa'NiDha♭PaMa↑Ga♭Re♭Sa
Vadi: Dha♭
Samvadi: Re♭
Jati: Sampurna–Sampurna
Profound contemplation, searching the soul. The tension between heavy Komal swaras and the raised Tivra Ma creates an unresolvable complexity — like a philosophical question with no easy answer.

Why is Todi so complex? Komal Re + Komal Ga creates heaviness. Tivra Ma cuts through it with brightness. Then Komal Dha pulls back down. This constant shift between dark (Komal) and bright (Tivra) creates the intellectually restless quality that defines Todi. The ear never settles — it is always being pulled in two directions simultaneously.

Raag Bihag
राग बिहाग
Bilawal Thaat
Night
9 PM–12 AM
★★★★★ Intermediate
What is this raag? Bihag is sweet, romantic, and unusually versatile — it uses both Shuddha Ma and Tivra Ma, giving it a playful, shifting quality. Famous film song "Lag Ja Gale" is based on Bihag. It is night's answer to daylight joy — warm rather than cool, intimate rather than grand.
Aaroh (note: no Re, no Dha going up)
SaGaMaMa↑PaNiSa'
Avaroh (full 7 notes)
Sa'NiDhaPaMaGaReSa
Vadi: Ga
Samvadi: Ni
Jati: Audav–Sampurna
Sweet, romantic, tender. Like a warm lamp in a dark room. The double Ma (both forms) creates a sense of playfulness and richness that makes Bihag immediately likeable.

Using both Shuddha Ma and Tivra Ma: Most raags use only one form of Ma. Bihag uses both — Shuddha Ma in the descent, Tivra Ma in the ascent. This gives the melody two different "colours" of Ma depending on direction, creating a multi-dimensional quality that other raags lack.

Raag Bageshri
राग बागेश्री
Kafi Thaat
Late Night
After midnight
★★★★★ Intermediate
What is this raag? Bageshri is the raag of deep devotional yearning — associated with Meera Bai's bhajans of longing for Krishna. Its distinctive feature: Re is absent going up but present going down, creating an upward-rushing quality that feels like reaching toward the divine. Often described as "waiting for a beloved who may not come."
Aaroh (no Re going up)
SaGa♭MaDhaNi♭Sa'
Avaroh (Re appears on the way down)
Sa'Ni♭DhaMaGa♭ReSa
Vadi: Ma
Samvadi: Sa
Jati: Audav–Shadav
Deep devotional longing, spiritual yearning, the ache of separation. Ma is the emotional centre — phrases constantly circle around it, never quite leaving.

Why does skipping Re going up create such longing? By jumping from Sa directly to Komal Ga (skipping Re), the melody creates an immediate, urgent upward surge. This "skip" creates the feeling of reaching without preparation — impulsive, emotionally direct longing. The full descent (including Re) is the calmer, more resigned return.

Raag Malkauns
राग मालकौंस
Bhairavi Thaat
Deep Night
12–3 AM
★★★★★ Intermediate
What is this raag? Malkauns is one of the most ancient and powerful raags — only 5 notes (all dark/Komal), no Re, no Pa. Legend says Tansen sang this raag to extinguish the fire that Raag Deepak had kindled. Its pentatonic minor structure is equivalent to the Western minor pentatonic scale — the same notes used in blues and rock music, which explains its universal appeal.
Aaroh (only 5 notes)
SaGa♭MaDha♭Ni♭Sa'
Avaroh
Sa'Ni♭Dha♭MaGa♭Sa
Vadi: Ma
Samvadi: Sa
Jati: Audav–Audav (only 5 notes)
Dark, mysterious, fierce, powerful. The three Komal swaras (Ga, Dha, Ni) with no Re and no Pa create an almost oppressive darkness. Not sad — powerful and awe-inspiring.

Why is Malkauns = Blues scale? Malkauns uses Sa-Ga♭-Ma-Dha♭-Ni♭. In the key of C: C-E♭-F-A♭-B♭. This is remarkably close to the Western minor pentatonic (C-E♭-F-G-B♭) and the blues scale. This is not coincidence — both systems independently discovered that this combination of darkened notes creates the most emotionally intense, universal human response.

Raag Marwa
राग मारवा
Marwa Thaat
Dusk
5–7 PM
★★★★★ Advanced
What is this raag? Marwa is the raag of suspended time — the last light of day before darkness. It is structurally unique: no Pa at all (the only "pillar" swara removed from a Sampurna raag), Komal Re, and Tivra Ma. This combination creates extreme harmonic tension — the music feels as if it can never quite resolve, like a question asked at the threshold of night.
Aaroh (no Pa)
SaRe♭GaMa↑DhaNiSa'
Avaroh (no Pa)
Sa'NiDhaMa↑GaRe♭Sa
Vadi: Re♭
Samvadi: Dha
Jati: Shadav–Shadav (6 notes, no Pa)
Restless, anxious, unfulfilled longing. The absence of Pa removes the natural "resting point" — the music is always searching, never fully settling. Like waiting for a train that may not come.

Why does removing Pa create such tension? Pa is the 5th harmonic — the most naturally consonant note after Sa and the upper Sa. Its absence means there is no comfortable mid-point to rest on. Combined with the harmonically unstable Tivra Ma and the low Komal Re, the raag creates an unresolvable harmonic tension that perfectly mirrors the feeling of dusk — caught between two worlds.

Reference

Swara Notation Guide

How to read the swar pills used throughout this library.

Ga — Shuddha (Natural)

Plain bordered pill = Shuddha (natural) swara. The "default" form of the note.

Ga♭ — Komal (Flat)

Red pill = Komal (flattened) swara. Available for: Re, Ga, Dha, Ni. Creates darker, more melancholic quality.

Ma↑ — Tivra (Sharp)

Orange pill = Tivra (sharpened) swara. Only Ma has a Tivra form. Creates brightness and anticipation.

Sa' — Upper Octave

Apostrophe = Taar Saptak (upper octave). Same note as Sa but one octave higher in pitch.
