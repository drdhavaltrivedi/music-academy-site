Hindustani Music Theory

A complete guide to the grammar, structure, and science behind Indian classical music

Swaras — स्वर

The Seven Notes of Hindustani Music

The word swara (स्वर) comes from the Sanskrit root meaning "that which shines by itself." Unlike notes in Western music, each swara in Hindustani tradition is believed to carry its own distinct emotional quality (rasa) and is associated with specific natural sounds, animals, and deities.

The seven primary swaras (Saptak) are: Sa, Re, Ga, Ma, Pa, Dha, Ni. Of these, Sa and Pa are achala (fixed/immovable) — they have no komal or tivra forms. The remaining five swaras each have multiple variants, creating a 12-tone musical system.

Swar Full Name Sanskrit Variants Natural Sound Origin Rasa (Emotion)
Sa Shadja षड्ज Fixed (achala) Peacock's cry Auspicious, foundational
Re Rishabh ऋषभ Komal Re, Shuddha Re Bull's call Pathos, longing (Komal); Joy (Shuddha)
Ga Gandhar गान्धार Komal Ga, Shuddha Ga Goat's bleat Tenderness, grief (Komal); Heroic (Shuddha)
Ma Madhyam मध्यम Shuddha Ma, Tivra Ma Heron's call Devotion (Shuddha); Yearning (Tivra)
Pa Pancham पञ्चम Fixed (achala) Cuckoo's song (Koyal) Love, heroism
Dha Dhaivat धैवत Komal Dha, Shuddha Dha Horse's neigh Tranquility (Shuddha); Deep longing (Komal)
Ni Nishad निषाद Komal Ni, Shuddha Ni Elephant's trumpet Courage (Shuddha); Melancholy (Komal)

When all 12 notes (7 Shuddha + 4 Komal + 1 Tivra) are counted, Hindustani music uses the same 12 pitches as Western equal temperament — but the tuning and emotional interpretation of each swara differs significantly.

Shrutis — श्रुति

The 22 Microtones of Indian Music

Ancient Indian musicology (as described in Bharata's Natyashastra, ~200 BCE) divides the octave into 22 shrutis — the smallest perceptible pitch intervals. Each swara occupies a specific group of shrutis, accounting for the fine nuances of intonation that distinguish Indian music from Western systems.

The 22 shruti system explains why Indian classical music sounds different even when the same 12 notes are used — a "Komal Ga" in Raag Todi is slightly flatter than a "Komal Ga" in Raag Bhimpalasi. This micro-intonation is not written but transmitted from Guru to Shishya.

Taal — ताल

The Rhythmic Cycles of Hindustani Music

Taal is the rhythmic framework — a repeating cycle of beats (matras) grouped into sections called vibhagas. Every performance in Hindustani music happens within a taal. The first beat of a cycle is called Sam (X), and the empty beats are called Khali (0). The Tabla player and vocalist both count from Sam back to Sam.

Three speeds (laya) are used: Vilambit (slow), Madhya (medium), and Drut (fast). The same taal can be played at all three speeds.

Teen Taal

16 matras
X Dha Dhin Dhin 2 Dha Dhin Dhin 0 Dha Tin Tin 3 Ta Dhin Dhin

The most common taal in khayal music. 4 vibhagas of 4 beats each. Used in all three speeds.

Ek Taal

12 matras
X Dhin Dhin 0 Dhin Dhin 2 Tin Tin 0 Dhin Dhin 3 Dhin Dhin 4 Dhin Dhin

6 vibhagas of 2 beats. Commonly used for Vilambit (slow) khayal. Also popular for Bhajan.

Jhap Taal

10 matras
X Dhi Na 2 Dhi Dhi Na 0 Ti Na 3 Dhi Dhi Na

Asymmetric — 2+3+2+3 structure. Gives a lilting, skipping quality to the rhythm.

Rupak Taal

7 matras
0 Tin Tin Na 2 Dhin Na 3 Dhin Na

Unique: Sam falls on Khali (the empty beat). Creates a dreamy, floating quality. 3+2+2 structure.

Keherwa Taal

8 matras
X Dha Ge Na Ti 0 Na Ka Dhi Na

The most common taal in semi-classical, bhajan, and film music. 2 vibhagas of 4 beats.

Dadra Taal

6 matras
X Dha Dhi Na 0 Dha Ti Na

Used for thumri, dadra, and Hori styles. Light, playful feel. 2 vibhagas of 3 beats each.

Char Taal

12 matras
X Dha Dha Dhin Ta 2 Kita Dha Dha 0 Dhin Ta Kita 3 Dha Dha Dhin

Used primarily in Dhrupad and khayal. Vigorous, driving rhythm with four vibhagas.

Deepchandi Taal

14 matras
X Dha Dhin – 2 Dha Dhin – 0 Ta Tin – 3 Ta Dhin –

4 vibhagas of 3+4+3+4 beats. Commonly used for Thumri in Vilambit laya.

Alankars — अलंकार

Melodic Exercises to Build Speed, Clarity, and Technique

Alankars (also called Paltas) are systematic melodic patterns used for daily vocal and instrumental practice. They develop muscle memory, finger dexterity (for instruments), clarity of swar, speed, and rhythmic accuracy. Every serious student begins practice with 30–60 minutes of alankar sadhana.

Alankars are practised in all three octaves (Mandra, Madhya, Taar) and at increasing tempos. Once mastered in a plain scale, they are applied within specific raags.

Alankar 1 — Basic Ascent-Descent (Aaroh-Avaroh)

S R G M P D N S' | S' N D P M G R S

The foundation of all practice. Establishes the basic scale pattern and ensures clarity of each swara in both directions.

Alankar 2 — Pairs (Yugal)

S-R R-G G-M M-P P-D D-N N-S' | reverse

Moving in connected pairs of swaras. Builds smooth transitions (portamento) and legato style.

Alankar 3 — Triplets (Teen-Teen)

S R G | R G M | G M P | M P D | P D N | D N S'

Groups of three swaras ascending. Fundamental to developing rhythmic triplet patterns in Teen Taal.

Alankar 4 — Quads (Chaar-Chaar)

S R G M | R G M P | G M P D | M P D N | P D N S'

Groups of four — the building block of bandish compositions. Aligns naturally with 4-beat vibhagas of Teen Taal.

Alankar 5 — Skip Pattern (Kootut)

S G R M | G P M D | P N D S'

Alternating skip pattern (1-3-2-4). Develops agility and the ability to navigate non-linear melodic movement within a raag.

Alankar 6 — Andolan (Oscillation)

S~R~ G~M~ P~D~ N~S'

Slow oscillation between adjacent swaras. Essential for developing gamak, meend, and the characteristic "andolan" ornament used in raags like Darbari Kanada.

Alankar 7 — Taan Pattern (Fast Run)

S R G M P G M P D M P D N P D N S' D N S'

Rapid cascading pattern. Foundation for the fast taan (melodic run) that forms the climax of a khayal performance.

Alankar 8 — Meend (Glide)

S ——→ Pa (slow glide) | Pa ——→ Sa

Smooth gliding between swaras without touching intermediate notes. The most expressive ornament in Hindustani vocal music — the hallmark of a refined gayaki.

10 Thaats — दस थाट

The Parent Scale System of Hindustani Music

Pandit Vishnu Narayan Bhatkhande (1860–1936) classified the hundreds of Hindustani raags into 10 thaats — parent scales, each with a distinct combination of 7 swaras. Every raag is derived from one of these thaats, similar to how Western modes derive from the major scale.

A thaat is not itself a raag — it is simply a set of 7 notes. The raag derived from it adds rules about aaroh-avaroh, vadi, time, gamak, and mood that give it its unique identity.

1. Bilawal

S R G M P D N — All Shuddha
Raags: Bilawal, Alhaiya Bilawal, Bihag

Equivalent to Western C major scale. The most natural, balanced thaat.

2. Kalyan

S R G M↑ P D N — Tivra Ma
Raags: Yaman, Bhupali, Kedar, Hameer

Only Ma is sharp. Creates the characteristic Lydian mood of evening raags.

3. Khamaj

S R G M P D N♭ — Komal Ni
Raags: Khamaj, Jhinjhoti, Des, Tilak Kamod

Light, romantic, semi-classical. Equivalent to Western Mixolydian.

4. Bhairav

S R♭ G M P D♭ N — Komal Re & Dha
Raags: Bhairav, Ramkali, Kalingda, Ahir Bhairav

Solemn, devotional morning thaat. Komal Re and Dha give deep gravitas.

5. Poorvi

S R♭ G M↑ P D♭ N — Komal Re, Dha + Tivra Ma
Raags: Poorvi, Shree, Puriya Dhanashri

Evening, majestic. The combination of Komal Re/Dha and Tivra Ma is unique and powerful.

6. Marwa

S R♭ G M↑ — D N — Komal Re + Tivra Ma, no Pa
Raags: Marwa, Sohini, Puriya

The only thaat that omits Pa. Creates suspended, restless yearning at dusk.

7. Kafi

S R G♭ M P D N♭ — Komal Ga & Ni
Raags: Kafi, Bhimpalasi, Bageshri, Dhani

Romantic, folk-like. Equivalent to Western Dorian mode. Base of Holi songs.

8. Asavari

S R G♭ M P D♭ N♭ — Komal Ga, Dha, Ni
Raags: Asavari, Darbari Kanada, Jaunpuri

Deep, majestic, sorrowful. Three Komal swaras give it heavy emotional weight.

9. Bhairavi

S R♭ G♭ M P D♭ N♭ — Four Komal swaras
Raags: Bhairavi, Malkauns (uses 5 of these)

The most melancholic thaat — 4 Komal notes. Often played as the closing piece of a concert.

10. Todi

S R♭ G♭ M↑ P D♭ N — Komal Re, Ga, Dha + Tivra Ma
Raags: Todi, Multani, Gurjari Todi

The most complex thaat — 3 Komal + 1 Tivra. Deep, searching, contemplative. King of morning raags.

Gamakas — गमक (Ornaments)

The Expressive Decorations of Hindustani Music

Gamakas are melodic ornaments that bring life and expression to swaras. A bare swara sung without gamaka is considered incomplete in Hindustani classical music. The masterful use of gamakas is what separates a polished artist from a student.

Gamaka Description Where Used
Meend A smooth, continuous glide from one swara to another without touching intermediate notes. The most important and expressive ornament. All raags, especially slow khayal
Gamak Rapid oscillation between two adjacent swaras with force and resonance. Different from andolan in its vigour. Darbari Kanada (Komal Ga), fast taans
Andolan Slow, gentle oscillation or wavering on a single swara. Creates a lingering, emotional effect. Raags like Darbari, Bhimpalasi
Khatka A quick grace note or slide from just above or below onto the target swara. Like a small flick. Semi-classical, thumri style
Murki A rapid cluster of 3–5 notes surrounding a central swara, executed quickly. Adds sparkle and life. Thumri, tappa, lighter styles
Kan Swar A touch or grace note — briefly touching a neighbouring swara before landing on the main note. Written as a small note. All styles; especially bandish
Zamzama A rapid hammering or repeated striking of the same swara, giving it a throbbing, trembling quality. Dhrupad, vigorous taans
Gitkari Quick, circular embellishment — a cascade of notes around a central tone, like a bird's trill. Thumri, fast passages

Gharanas — घराना

Schools of Hindustani Music

A gharana (from "ghar" meaning house/family) is a school of music transmission — a lineage of masters passing their distinctive musical style from teacher to student over generations. Each gharana has its own characteristic approach to voice production, ornament use, tempo, and composition style. Knowing a performer's gharana immediately tells an informed listener what to expect.

Gharana City Style Characteristics Notable Artists
Gwalior Gwalior, MP Oldest khayal gharana. Clear, powerful voice, symmetrical compositions, methodical alaap, emphasis on sur shuddhata (purity of pitch). Vishnu Digambar Paluskar, Kumar Gandharva
Kirana Kirana, Haryana Slow, meditative, deeply emotive alaap. Emphasis on "bhav" (feeling) over speed. Extensive use of meend and andolan. Khali-pili (plain) gayaki. Abdul Karim Khan, Gangubai Hangal, Bhimsen Joshi
Agra Agra, UP Powerful, vigorous "dhamar" ang (Dhrupad influence). Nom-tom alaap, forceful gamak, large compositions. Bold and masculine style. Faiyaz Khan, Latafat Hussain Khan
Jaipur-Atrauli Jaipur, Rajasthan Complex rhythmic interplay, unusual raags, fast layakari. Emphasis on taal mastery alongside melody. Technically demanding. Alladiya Khan, Kishori Amonkar, Mallikarjun Mansur
Patiala Patiala, Punjab Blend of Dhrupad and khayal with thumri elements. Rich use of murki, gitkari, and tappa ornaments. Romantic, expressive style. Bade Ghulam Ali Khan, Barkat Ali Khan
Rampur-Sahaswan UP Influence of Senia tradition (Tansen lineage). Clear articulation of notes, moderate tempo, elaborate taan patterns. Inayat Hussain Khan, Rashid Khan
Mewati Rajasthan/Haryana Devotional content (bhakti), heavily influenced by Bhairavi and Yaman. Blend of khayal and thumri with spiritual quality. Pandit Jasraj
