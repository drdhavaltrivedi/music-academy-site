Music Theory · Complete Guide

The Grammar of Hindustani Music

Every art form has a grammar. Hindustani music's grammar is 3,000 years deep — swaras, shrutis, thaats, taals, and ornaments. This page explains all of it in plain language.

Swaras — स्वर

The Seven Musical Notes and Their 12 Forms

If you have read the Foundations page, you already know that a swara is a stable musical pitch, and that there are 7 primary swaras: Sa Re Ga Ma Pa Dha Ni. This section goes deeper — into why each swara has the character it does, and how the 12-note system works.

🎨

Swaras are like primary colours

A painter has 3 primary colours (red, blue, yellow) from which all other colours are mixed. A Hindustani musician has 7 primary swaras, from which all raags, all emotions, all musical worlds are built. Just as mixing blue and yellow gives infinite shades of green, combining swaras in specific ways gives infinite emotional textures.

Here is the complete 12-note breakdown — the "full palette" every student must know:

The two Achala swaras — Sa and Pa

Sa and Pa are called Achala (अचल — immovable) because they have no Komal or Tivra variants. They are the two pillars of the harmonic system:

Sa is the 1st harmonic (the root). Pa is the 3rd harmonic (a perfect fifth above Sa), and it is the strongest natural resonance in music. In Western terms, if Sa is C, Pa is G — the most consonant interval after the octave. This is why Pa always sounds "settled" and "supportive" against Sa, regardless of the raag.

This also explains why beginner exercises focus on Sa and Pa — you are training on the two most stable, resonant points in the entire harmonic system.

Shrutis — श्रुति

The 22 Microtones — The "Pixels" of Indian Sound

Most students know the 7 swaras and their 12 forms. But Indian musicology goes much further — it recognises 22 distinct pitches within one octave, called Shrutis. This is described in detail in Bharata's Natyashastra, one of the oldest treatises on performing arts (approximately 200 BCE).

🖥️

Think of shrutis like pixels

A computer screen displays millions of colours, but a painter working with paint only uses a few dozen — the rest would be imperceptible in practice. Similarly, the human ear can distinguish approximately 22 meaningfully different pitches in one octave, even though theoretically infinite pitches exist. The 22 shrutis are the "pixels" of Indian music — the finest level of pitch that is musically meaningful.

The 12 notes we normally discuss are the most prominent of the 22 shrutis. Each of the 12 notes occupies a "band" of 1–2 shrutis. The exact placement within that band varies between raags — this is why a "Komal Ga" in Raag Todi is played slightly lower than a "Komal Ga" in Raag Bhimpalasi. A trained musician's ear perceives this difference; an untrained ear may not.

Why does the 22-shruti system matter practically?

It explains why Hindustani music cannot be perfectly reproduced on a keyboard — the piano has fixed, equal-tempered notes (12 per octave, evenly spaced). Hindustani music uses slightly "bent" pitches that don't fit into the piano's equal temperament. This is why sitar, sarangi, and the human voice sound richer and more expressive in this music — they can produce any of the 22 shrutis continuously.

The Andolan (oscillation) connects shrutis

When a vocalist uses "andolan" — a slow, deliberate wavering on a note — they are literally moving between adjacent shrutis. In Raag Darbari Kanada, the Komal Ga is performed with a characteristic andolan between two nearby shrutis, creating the distinctive "searching" quality unique to that raag. This cannot be written in notation — it is transmitted ear-to-ear from Guru to student.

For a beginner: you don't need to memorise the 22 shrutis. But understanding that they exist explains why you must always practice with a Guru and a Tanpura — written notation cannot capture the fine intonation, and only a human teacher can correct your shruti placement.

Taals — ताल

Rhythmic Cycles — The Heartbeat of Hindustani Music

If you are coming from the Foundations page, you already know that a Taal is a repeating cycle of beats. Here we go deeper — explaining every major taal, how to count it, and what feeling it creates. You also learn about Vibhag, Sam, Khali, and the three speeds (Layas).

Sam — The Most Important Beat

Beat 1 of every taal cycle is called Sam (सम). It is marked with "X" in notation. In performance, both the vocalist and the Tabla player have complete freedom to improvise — but they must meet on Sam. Missing Sam is the equivalent of a grammatical error. Arriving on Sam after a complex improvisation is the peak dramatic moment of a Hindustani performance.

Khali — The Empty Beat

Khali (खाली — empty) is the "anti-Sam" — a special beat where the Tabla plays a lighter sound and the clapping pattern changes from a clap to a wave. It marks the midpoint of most taals and creates tension. The music "breathes out" on Khali and "breathes in" toward Sam again.

Three Speeds — Laya

Vilambit (slow), Madhya (medium), Drut (fast). The same raag and bandish can be performed at all three speeds. Typically a performance starts slow (alaap + Vilambit), builds to medium, then ends with fast Drut. The contrast in speeds creates drama.

Vibhag — Groups Within the Cycle

The beats are grouped into sections called Vibhag. These groups are marked by the clapping pattern. Teen Taal has four Vibhags of 4 beats each. Rupak Taal has three Vibhags (3+2+2). The Vibhag structure gives each taal its rhythmic "flavour".

Teen Taal — तीन ताल Most Common

16 beats · 4 vibhags of 4
X: Dha Dhin Dhin Dha 2: Dha Dhin Dhin Dha 0: Dha Tin Tin Ta 3: Ta Dhin Dhin Dha

The "mother" of all taals in khayal music. Symmetrical and clear — perfect for learning. The Khali falls on beat 9, exactly halfway through the cycle.

Used for: Khayal (all speeds), Bhajan, semi-classical music

Ek Taal — एक ताल

12 beats · 6 vibhags of 2
X: Dhin Dhin 0: Dhin Dhin 2: Tin Tin 0: Dhin Dhin 3: Dhin Dhin 4: Dhin Dhin

Deceptively complex for a beginner — two vibhags sound identical (Dhin Dhin) but have different functions. Has two Khalis, making it hard to track. Used in Vilambit (slow) khayal.

Used for: Slow khayal, bhajans

Jhap Taal — झप ताल

10 beats · 4 vibhags: 2+3+2+3
X: Dhi Na 2: Dhi Dhi Na 0: Ti Na 3: Dhi Dhi Na

The asymmetric 2+3+2+3 grouping gives it a "limping" or skipping quality — like a gentle wave. Very popular in semi-classical and folk-influenced music. Much easier to learn than Ek Taal.

Used for: Semi-classical khayal, bhajan

Rupak Taal — रूपक ताल

7 beats · 3 vibhags: 3+2+2
0: Tin Tin Na 2: Dhin Na 3: Dhin Na

Unique and counterintuitive: Sam (beat 1) falls on Khali — the "empty" beat. This reversal of expectation creates a floating, dream-like quality. The melody seems to drift without a clear anchor.

Used for: Thumri, light classical, bhajan

Keherwa Taal — कहरवा ताल

8 beats · 2 vibhags of 4
X: Dha Ge Na Ti 0: Na Ka Dhi Na

The most "common" taal you will encounter in everyday life — almost all Bollywood and folk songs use Keherwa or its variants. Light, bouncy, and immediately recognisable. Very easy to learn first.

Used for: Bhajan, thumri, folk, film music

Dadra Taal — दादरा ताल

6 beats · 2 vibhags of 3
X: Dha Dhi Na 0: Dha Ti Na

The waltz of Indian music — 3+3 creates a rolling, gentle sway. Named after the Dadra style of semi-classical singing. Used in Holi songs, bhajans, and thumri. Very natural and easy to feel.

Used for: Dadra gayaki, Hori, thumri, bhajan

Char Taal — चार ताल

12 beats · 6 vibhags: 2+2+2+2+2+2
X: Dha Dha 0: Dhin Ta 2: Kita Dha 0: Dhin Ta 3: Tita Kata 4: Gadi Gana

The taal of Dhrupad — the oldest and most austere style of Hindustani music. Vigorous, driving, majestic. Two Khalis. The "kita" syllables show quick ornamentation on the Tabla.

Used for: Dhrupad, Dhamar (temple music)

Deepchandi Taal — दीपचंदी ताल

14 beats · 4 vibhags: 3+4+3+4
X: Dha Dhin – 2: Dha Dhin – – 0: Ta Tin – 3: Ta Dhin – –

The most common taal for Thumri (the romantic, expressive semi-classical style). Asymmetric 3+4+3+4 creates a gentle sway, like a boat on water. Deeply evocative, ideal for emotional expression.

Used for: Thumri, Dadra, romantic compositions

The 10 Thaats — दस थाट

The Parent Scale System — How All Raags Are Organised

In the early 20th century, Pandit Vishnu Narayan Bhatkhande faced a problem: hundreds of raags existed, but there was no systematic way to classify them. He created the 10 Thaat system — classifying every raag under one of 10 parent scales.

🌳

Think of Thaats like family trees

A thaat is like a family name — it tells you which "family" a raag belongs to. Raag Yaman, Raag Bhupali, and Raag Kedar are all in the "Kalyan family" (Kalyan Thaat). They share their characteristic Tivra Ma, but each has its own personality, rules, and emotional world.

A Thaat is simply a set of 7 notes. It is not a raag itself — it has no aaroh-avaroh rules, no vadi, no time. It is only the raw material from which raags are crafted. Think of it like a painter's palette: the palette doesn't create art — the artist's specific choices do.

Thaat 1

Bilawal

S R G M P D N

Raags: Bilawal, Bihag, Alhaiya Bilawal

All Shuddha swaras — identical to the Western C major scale. The "natural" thaat, bright and open. Raags here feel cheerful and fresh.

Thaat 2

Kalyan

S R G M↑ P D N

Raags: Yaman, Bhupali, Kedar, Hameer

Only Ma is raised (Tivra). Creates a bright, anticipatory, slightly Lydian mood. The most popular evening thaat — Yaman is India's most-taught raag.

Thaat 3

Khamaj

S R G M P D N♭

Raags: Khamaj, Des, Jhinjhoti, Tilak Kamod

Komal Ni only. Light, romantic, close to folk music. Equivalent to the Western Mixolydian mode. These raags have a sweet, accessible character — very common in film music.

Thaat 4

Bhairav

S R♭ G M P D♭ N

Raags: Bhairav, Ramkali, Kalingda, Ahir Bhairav

Komal Re and Komal Dha. Solemn, devotional, meditative. Prescribed for early morning. Named after Shiva in his fierce form. Deep spiritual gravity.

Thaat 5

Poorvi

S R♭ G M↑ P D♭ N

Raags: Poorvi, Shree, Puriya Dhanashri

Komal Re, Komal Dha, AND Tivra Ma. A complex, powerful combination. Creates majestic grandeur at evening time — sunset raags that feel enormous and solemn.

Thaat 6

Marwa

S R♭ G M↑ — D N

Raags: Marwa, Sohini, Puriya

The only thaat without Pa. Komal Re + Tivra Ma + no Pa = extreme tension, restlessness, unfulfilled longing. These raags create a suspended feeling — like waiting for something that hasn't arrived.

Thaat 7

Kafi

S R G♭ M P D N♭

Raags: Kafi, Bhimpalasi, Bageshri, Dhani

Komal Ga and Komal Ni — the "minor" sound of Indian music. Romantic, folk-like, used for Holi songs and thumri. Equivalent to the Western Dorian mode.

Thaat 8

Asavari

S R G♭ M P D♭ N♭

Raags: Asavari, Darbari Kanada, Jaunpuri

Komal Ga, Dha, and Ni — three Komal swaras. Heavy, dignified, and sorrowful. Darbari Kanada (created by Tansen for Akbar's court) is the supreme expression of this thaat.

Thaat 9

Bhairavi

S R♭ G♭ M P D♭ N♭

Raags: Bhairavi, Malkauns (pentatonic)

Four Komal swaras — the most melancholic thaat. Bhairavi is traditionally the last piece performed at a concert — the farewell, full of longing and acceptance. Its richness comes from using all possible Komal colours.

Thaat 10

Todi

S R♭ G♭ M↑ P D♭ N

Raags: Todi, Multani, Gurjari Todi

Three Komal swaras AND Tivra Ma — the most complex combination. Deep, searching, restless intellectualism. Raag Todi is considered the "king of morning raags" and a mark of advanced mastery.

Gamakas — गमक

Ornaments — The Emotion Embedded in Every Note

A note in Hindustani music is rarely sung "plain" — bare, unadorned, in isolation. Instead, every important note is approached, held, and left with specific gestures called Gamakas (गमक). These ornaments are not decoration — they are the emotion itself. The same note can sound joyful, tragic, or mysterious depending purely on how it is ornamented.

🗣️

Think of gamakas like tone in speech

Say the word "really?" as a surprised question. Now say "really." as a flat, bored statement. Now "REALLY!" as an excited exclamation. The word is the same; the tone completely changes its meaning. Gamakas do the same thing to musical notes — the swara is the word, the gamaka is the tone of voice.

GamakaWhat it IsHow to Produce ItEmotional EffectUsed In
Meend
(मींड)		 A smooth, continuous glide from one swara to another — without touching any notes in between. Keep the throat open and voice connected throughout. Do not lift the voice or break contact. Imagine pulling silk. Liquid, flowing, deeply expressive. Creates a sense of continuity and emotional depth. All raags, especially slow khayal. The most important gamaka.
Andolan
(आंदोलन)		 A slow, deliberate oscillation or wavering on a single swara — staying close to the note but gently swaying around it. Settle on the note, then gently waver about a quarter-tone above and below, at a slow pace. Like a tree in a gentle breeze. Deep sorrow, spiritual longing, introspection. Creates a haunted, profound quality. Raag Darbari Kanada (Komal Ga), Bhimpalasi, morning raags.
Gamak
(गमक)		 Rapid, forceful oscillation between two swaras — like a powerful trill. More vigorous than Andolan. Quick, powerful back-and-forth between two adjacent notes. Requires breath force and vocal strength. Power, energy, grandeur, heroism. Used in climactic moments. Fast taans, Dhrupad, vigorous khayal passages.
Khatka
(खटका)		 A quick grace note or "flick" from just above or below onto the target swara. Very brief. Touch the neighbouring note lightly and immediately land on the target. Like a light tap on a piano key before the main key. Playfulness, liveliness, spontaneity. Adds a sparkling quality. Thumri, semi-classical styles. Very common in film music.
Murki
(मुरकी)		 A rapid cluster of 3–5 notes circling around a central swara — like a fast, localised trill. Quickly pass through 3–4 nearby notes and land on the target. Must be fast and clean — sounds like a brief bird call. Delicacy, sophistication, wit. Like an musical exclamation point. Thumri, tappa, ornamental passages in khayal.
Kan Swara
(कण स्वर)		 A "touch note" — briefly touching a neighbouring swara before landing on the main note. Written as a small note in notation. Like whispering the grace note — barely audible but present. The main note gets all the weight. Subtle expressiveness, adds colour without drama. The "seasoning" of melody. All styles. Especially common in bandish compositions.
Zamzama
(जमजमा)		 Rapid, hammered repetition of the same swara — a throbbing, vibrating effect on one note. Re-attack the same note very quickly, repeatedly. Creates a trembling, urgent quality. Urgency, tension, powerful invocation. Like a heartbeat accelerating. Dhrupad, vigorous taans, dramatic passages.
Gitkari
(गिटकारी)		 A circular, swirling embellishment — multiple notes cascading around a centre tone. Like a trill expanding outward. Begin at the centre note, swirl upward and downward through 4–6 nearby notes, return to centre. Fast and fluid. Exuberance, celebration, richness of sound. Thumri, fast khayal, ornamental passages.

Gharanas — घराना

Schools of Music — Why Two Singers Sound Completely Different Singing the Same Raag

You may have heard two different singers perform Raag Yaman and felt they sounded completely different — same notes, but entirely different worlds. This is the effect of different Gharanas (घराना — "house" or "family").

A Gharana is a lineage of musical transmission — a style passed from Guru to student over multiple generations. Each Gharana has its own characteristic approach to voice production, ornament use, tempo, bandish style, and philosophical attitude toward music. When a Guru transmits music to a student, they transmit not just notes but an entire worldview about how music should feel.

🍳

Think of gharanas like regional cuisines

North India has many regional cuisines — all use similar ingredients (rice, dal, spices) but each region prepares them completely differently. You can tell Punjabi food from Bengali food immediately, even if both contain the same ingredients. Gharanas are the same: all use the same swaras and raags, but the preparation — the texture, flavour, emotional emphasis — is unmistakably different.

GharanaOriginMusical CharacterFamous Artists
Gwalior Gwalior, Madhya Pradesh The oldest khayal gharana. Clear, powerful voice production. Emphasis on sur shuddhi (pitch purity) above all else. Symmetrical, well-structured compositions. Methodical alaap — note by note, slowly revealing the raag. Conservative and foundational. Vishnu Digambar Paluskar, Krishnarao Shankar Pandit, Kumar Gandharva
Kirana Kirana, Haryana Slow, deeply meditative alaap. Emphasis on bhav (feeling) over speed or complexity. Extensive use of meend and andolan. The voice seems to breathe the raag, not perform it. Strongly associated with spiritual music-making. Abdul Karim Khan, Gangubai Hangal, Bhimsen Joshi, Prabha Atre
Agra Agra, Uttar Pradesh Powerful, vigorous style with Dhrupad influence. "Nom-tom" alaap (starting with syllables like "nom" before words). Forceful gamak, large bandish structures. Bold, masculine, dramatic. Rooted in temple music traditions. Faiyaz Khan, Latafat Hussain Khan, Sharafat Hussain Khan
Jaipur-Atrauli Jaipur, Rajasthan Technically demanding. Complex rhythmic interplay with the Tabla, unusual raags rarely heard in other gharanas. Emphasis on Layakari (rhythmic play). ✓ How to use Gharana knowledge as a student

Don't worry about choosing a Gharana until you have a Guru. When you study under a Guru, you will naturally absorb their Gharana's approach. As a listener, knowing Gharanas helps you identify why the same raag feels completely different from two different artists. Listen to Raag Yaman by Kishori Amonkar (Jaipur-Atrauli) and then by Bhimsen Joshi (Kirana) — you will hear two entirely different emotional worlds in the same raag.
